More than 1 million immigrants arrive in the U.S. each year.

Without domestic credit history, a recent immigrant is not allowed access to domestic credit products. They are expected to start over -- even when they might have a perfectly good financial history abroad.

Let's tear down that wall.

In 2015 European Union adopted the Payment Services Directive (PSD2) to facilitate competition and encourage non-banks to participate in the payments industry -- and ultimately to create a more integrated European payments market. This had practical implications beyond the borders of Europe -- world-wide, in fact, including North America. This spurred the development of the Open Banking initiative, which includes open APIs to provide more transparency into personal financial data (with managed consent, of course).

The emergence of Open Banking created an "economy of scale" effect which we can leverage to solve the portable financial history problem. We mean that, instead of integrating with each bank, we can now access data from many banks in an entire region (e.g. Europe) via open banking data facilitators, like Plaid, Tink, and Brankas. But furthermore we can see that even those services are balkanized and de-normalized, so that here is a higher-level opportunity.

We are solving the problem of credit score portability. For example, in the United States it takes an immigrant 1+ year to access the credit products that people with local credit score have access to. Most immigrants have credit histories abroad which are not taken into account by lenders in the United States at all. It is untenable in 2021.

In a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) we provide recent Europe -> United States immigrants with a credit score calculated based on their credit history from Europe, which lending businesses in the US can access and incorporate in their loan decision-making process.

Our long-term goal is to allow a person who is immigrating to a different country to take their domestic credit history with them and be able to use it to access credit products in a country they are moving to -- or rather, to make it so any person has a unified credit history regardless of borders.
